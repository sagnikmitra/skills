# Genre structures

Sagnik writes in a few recurring shapes. Pick the one that fits, then fill it in his voice.

## Explainer / "research, said plainly" (e.g. the harness page)

1. **Opener** — pre-empt the reader's objection, then pivot to your thesis. First-person.
2. **The one idea** — state the core claim plainly (a pull-quote works), no zinger.
3. **Definitions** — a short vocabulary section so terms are shared. One tiny analogy is fine.
4. **The model, section by section** — each concept gets: a plain explanation → a teaching analogy → a figure/table/code if it earns one → the honest caveat.
5. **Where it pays off** — the practical, commercial application.
6. **The rules** — a numbered list of "what I try to follow", humble framing.
7. **So, in the end** — restate the thesis, a closing pull-quote, a gentle sign-off, a hand-off link to the fuller source, References.

Depth markers: real schemas, a formula, named tools, concrete numbers. Hand off to the paper/repo with dashed "→ in the paper" links.

## Tutorial (e.g. Streamlit, Snake game)

1. **Opener** — a plain question or "what is rare to find is...", state honestly what this does and its limits up front.
2. **Dependencies / environment** — what you need, and the environment quirks ("in windows... it is Shift + Ins").
3. **Step by step** — for *each* code block: a plain-English explanation of what it does *before* the code, then the code, then (optionally) a screenshot.
4. **Caveats** — the honest drawbacks ("minor flickering issue, it works fine").
5. **Close** — "And that's it." → practical next step → source repo link → "do follow me on GitHub" → "Do give this a clap if you like it."

## Review (e.g. phone reviews)

1. **Opener** — thesis-first enthusiasm or pre-empt ("You will say that why a 2018 phone in 2020").
2. **Fixed section skeleton** — Design → Camera → Display → Charging → Software/UI → Security → Performance → Battery.
3. Each section: lived first-person testing ("in my usage"), exact specs/numbers, a balanced read, sources credited under any image.
4. **Verdict** — the honest summary, gated on who it is for.
5. **Pricing & buying link** — practical, concrete.

## Narrative / explainer-story (e.g. Tucker's Cross)

1. **Scene-setting / grand frame** opener.
2. Chronological telling, dramatic but plain; pull-quotes from sources.
3. Honest about uncertainty ("we tried to present it as transparently as possible, there are many conflicts").
4. **References list with URLs** as the close.

## Personal reflection (e.g. Hacking Community)

1. **"This is my story..."** frame, humble.
2. Chronological, concrete, a little awkward and honest ("I spent my first two years of College just roaming around").
3. Credit the people who helped.
4. Lyrical, soft closer ("Enjoyed, Enjoying and will Enjoy much much more…").

## Research-page (sgnk.ai) — special case

Deploy.", PDF + cite blocks, "→ in the paper" deep-links, and the harness page as the worked reference. Keep the prose in his voice; let the page be polished even though the voice stays plain.
